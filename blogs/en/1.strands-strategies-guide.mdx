## Why Strategy Matters in Strands

NYT Strands isn't just about vocabulary — it's about spatial thinking, theme interpretation, and systematic grid scanning. The best solvers don't just know more words; they think more strategically about how to decode the theme clue and navigate the 6×8 letter grid.

Here are 10 strategies that will immediately improve your solve rate.

## 1. Read the Theme Clue Carefully

Before scanning the grid, spend time thinking about the theme clue. Consider multiple interpretations — the clue might be a pun, an idiom, or a double meaning. If the clue is "On a roll," the theme words might be types of rolls (bread, drum, egg) rather than things that roll.

**Tip:** Brainstorm 5-10 words that could fit the theme before looking at the grid.

## 2. Find the Spangram First

The Spangram is the key word that spans the entire board from one edge to the opposite edge, highlighted in gold. It encapsulates the overall theme. Finding it first confirms your interpretation of the clue and makes all other theme words easier to spot.

**How to find it:** Trace paths from one edge of the board to the opposite edge, looking for long words (7-12 letters) that relate to the theme.

## 3. Use the Corners and Edges

Corner letters only connect to 3 adjacent letters, and edge letters connect to 5. This makes them easier starting points than interior letters (which connect to 8). Start your word searches from corners and edges to reduce the number of paths you need to check.

## 4. Remember: Words Can Twist and Turn

Unlike traditional word searches where words travel in straight lines, Strands words can change direction at every letter. A 6-letter word might snake through the grid, turning left, right, up, down, or diagonally at each step. Don't limit yourself to straight-line scanning.

## 5. Track Uncovered Letters

As you find theme words and the Spangram, notice which letters are "used up." Every letter on the board belongs to exactly one answer word. As you eliminate letters, the remaining uncovered letters must form the remaining answers — dramatically narrowing your search.

## 6. Earn Hint Tokens Strategically

When you find a valid non-theme word (at least 4 letters), you earn a hint token. Every 3 tokens reveals a letter in a theme word. Don't view this as a penalty — it's a designed game mechanic. If you're stuck for more than 2-3 minutes, start hunting for non-theme words to earn hints.

## 7. Look for Common Letter Patterns

Scan the grid for common English letter combinations:

- **Beginnings:** TH, SH, CH, ST, PR, TR
- **Endings:** -ING, -TION, -ED, -ER, -LY, -MENT
- **Pairs:** QU, CK, PH, WH

When you spot these patterns, trace outward to see if they form complete theme words.

## 8. Use Process of Elimination

When you're down to the last 2-3 words, count the remaining uncovered letters and divide by the number of remaining words. This tells you the approximate length of each remaining word. If 10 letters remain and you need 2 words, look for a 5-letter and 5-letter word, or a 4-letter and 6-letter word.

## 9. Consider Word Variants

Theme words might be plural, past tense, or use other word forms you wouldn't expect. If the theme is about cooking, look for BAKING, GRILLED, and STEAMED — not just BAKE, GRILL, and STEAM.

## 10. Review Past Puzzles

The Strands puzzle follows patterns in theme types and clue styles. By reviewing past puzzles in our [archive](/strands-hint), you'll start recognizing common theme patterns (foods, animals, wordplay) and clue techniques (puns, double meanings). The more puzzles you study, the faster you'll decode new ones.
