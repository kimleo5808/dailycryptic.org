## What is NYT Strands?

NYT Strands is a daily word puzzle published by The New York Times. It combines elements of word searches and crossword puzzles into a unique grid-based challenge that has quickly become one of the most popular daily word games.

Each day, you're presented with a 6×8 grid of letters. Your goal is to find all the theme words hidden in the grid by connecting adjacent letters. Every puzzle has a theme clue, several theme words, and a special Spangram word that spans the entire board.

## How the Game Works

### The Grid

When you open the puzzle, you see 48 letters arranged in a 6-column, 8-row grid. Unlike traditional word searches, every single letter belongs to exactly one answer word — there are no leftover or filler letters.

### The Theme Clue

At the top of the puzzle, you'll see a theme clue. This hint tells you what all the theme words have in common. The clue might be straightforward ("Fruits") or involve wordplay ("On a roll").

### Finding Words

Connect adjacent letters (horizontally, vertically, or diagonally) to form words. Words can twist and turn in any direction — they don't have to follow straight lines. Each letter can only be used once per word.

### Theme Words (Blue)

When you find a word that matches the puzzle's theme, it's highlighted in blue and those letters are marked as "found." There are typically 6-8 theme words per puzzle.

### The Spangram (Gold)

The Spangram is a special word that spans the entire board — it must touch two opposite edges (left-right, top-bottom, or diagonally). It's highlighted in gold and describes the overall theme. Think of it as the category name for all the theme words.

### Hint System

Finding valid non-theme words (at least 4 letters long) earns you hint tokens. Every 3 tokens reveals the location of a letter in one of the remaining theme words. This is a built-in help mechanic — use it when you're stuck.

### Winning

You win when you find the Spangram and all theme words. The entire grid will be covered, with every letter belonging to an answer word.

## Step-by-Step: Your First Puzzle

Here's how to approach your first Strands puzzle:

**Step 1:** Read the theme clue. Think about what words might relate to it. Don't rush to the grid yet.

**Step 2:** Scan the grid for the Spangram. Look for long words that stretch from one edge to the opposite edge. The Spangram describes the theme, so finding it confirms your interpretation.

**Step 3:** Look for obvious theme words. Start from corners and edges where letter connections are simpler.

**Step 4:** As you find words, track which letters are "used." The remaining uncovered letters must form the remaining answers.

**Step 5:** If stuck, find any valid 4+ letter words (even non-theme words) to earn hint tokens. Three tokens reveal a letter.

**Step 6:** Use elimination. As fewer letters remain, the possible words become easier to identify.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Straight-Line Scanning

The biggest beginner mistake is looking for words in straight lines only. Strands words snake through the grid, changing direction at every letter. Think of it like tracing a path on a map, not reading text on a page.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the Theme Clue

Jumping straight into word-hunting without considering the clue wastes time. The clue tells you what types of words to look for. Always start by thinking about the theme.

### Mistake 3: Avoiding the Hint System

Many beginners see hints as "cheating." They're not — they're a designed part of the game. If you've been stuck for 3+ minutes, earn some hint tokens. It's much better than staring at the grid in frustration.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting the Spangram

Some beginners focus entirely on finding theme words and forget about the Spangram. The Spangram is often the key to understanding the theme. Finding it early gives you a major advantage.

### Mistake 5: Not Tracking Used Letters

After finding a word, its letters are "used up" — they belong to that word and not to any other. The remaining uncovered letters form the remaining answers. Pay attention to what's left on the board.
